The Harmonic Oscillator Model

At any given time, the potential energy of a system is given by:

$\begin{eqnarray*} F &=& -kx \\ ma &=& -kx \\ m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} &=& -kx \\ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} &=& \frac{-k}{m} x \\ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \frac{k}{m} x &=& 0 \\ x(t) &=& ... \\ \end{eqnarray*}$

(you can solve that)

The potential energy of the system is then $V(x) = \frac{1}{2} kx^2$.

The potential energy operator is just $\hat{V} = \frac{1}{2} kx^2$.

After a crunchy amount of maths, you'll end up with this:

$E = (v + \frac{1}{2}) h\nu$

Noticing that $E_{min} = \frac{1}{2}h\nu$. Why is that?

Zero Point Energy (or the right name for it)

The ground state of a particle has $\frac{1}{2}h\nu$ energy. Even if the molecule has no energy to translate (move), it still has energy to vibrate at 0K.

Tunnelling

Tunnelling occurs in the Harmonic Oscillator model.
The wavefunction is non-zero outside the $\frac{1}{2} kx^2$ parabola, which somehow explains ammonia's nitrogen inversion.

Equations for the Harmonic Oscillator model

  • $\lt x \gt = 0$ (odd function)
  • $\lt p \gt = 0$ (odd function)
  • $\lt x^2 \gt = \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{mk}} (v + \frac{1}{2})$
  • $\lt p^2 \gt = \hbar\sqrt{mk} (v + \frac{1}{2})$
  • $\lt T \gt = \lt \frac{p^2}{2m} \gt = \frac{\hbar}{2} \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}(v+\frac{1}{2})$
  • $\lt V \gt = \frac{\hbar}{2} \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}(v+\frac{1}{2})$ (the same thing)
  • $\lt E \gt = \lt T \gt + \lt V \gt = \hbar \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}(v+\frac{1}{2})$
