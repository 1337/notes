Points

  • A point: $\lt x_0, y_0, z_0 \gt$
  • x-value of a point: the closest distance to the y-z plane
  • Distance between two points $P_1$ and $P_2$: $|P_1 P_2| = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2 + (z_2-z_1)^2}$
  • Line between two points: $\bar{AB} = \lt B_x - A_x, B_y - A_y, B_z - A_z \gt$

Lines

  • Equation of a line: $\bar{r} = \bar{r_0} + \bar{P_0P} = \bar{r_0} + t\bar{v}$
    where $\bar{r_0} = \lt r_x, r_y, r_z \gt$, $t$ is the scalar multiplier, and $\bar{v} = \lt a, b, c \gt$

    Since $x = x_0 + at, y = y_0 + bt, z = z_0 + ct$, $\frac{x - x_0}{a} = \frac{y - y_0}{b} = \frac{z - z_0}{c}$ ($a, b, c$ being direction numbers)
  • Length of a line: $|a| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}$

Planes

A plane is made of a normal vector $\bar{n}$, which is perpendicular to a line inside the plane, $\bar{P_0P}$.

That means $\bar{n} \cdot \bar{P_0P} \cos{\theta} = \bar{n} \cdot \bar{P_0P} (0) = 0$

Directional Derivatives

$F_x$ is slope along x and $F_y$ is slope along y.
We can have a $F_s = (X_s, Y_s)$ where $S$ is a unit vector of $(\cos \theta, \sin \theta)$.

Example: we want directional derivative of Z along S:
$\begin{eqnarray*} D_{\hat{u}}f &=& \frac{dz}{ds} \\ &=& \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} \frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} \frac{\partial y}{\partial s} \\ &=& F_x \frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + F_y \frac{\partial y}{\partial s} \\ &=& F_x \cos (\theta) + F_y \sin (\theta) \\ &=& (F_x, F_y) \cdot \hat{u} \\ &=& \nabla{\vec{f}} \cdot \hat{u} \end{eqnarray*}$

Multiple Integrals

Over a region $D$ (also $E$ or $R$) = $\{(x,y) | g_1(x) \leq y \leq g_2(x),\ a \leq x \leq b \}$

$\iint\limits_S z\ dA = \int\limits^{y=\inf}_{y=-\inf} \int\limits^{x=f_{over}(y)}_{x=f_{under}(y)} f(x,y)\ dx\ dy $

If all bounds are consts, order doesn't matter. If a set of bounds e.g. y is a func of x, do y first.
Note that changing integration order will change the integration limits.
