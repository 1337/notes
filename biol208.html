Approaches to studying genetics

Genetic approach

  • Studies physical property of an organism first.
  • Studies inheritable trait of a given organism, e.g. how plants bend towards light and sleeping patterns in mice.
    • You can intentionally create mutations using chemicals (EMS), radiation (UV), or transposons.

Forward genetics

Observe a random change in phenotype. Find the gene involved.

Phenotype => Genotype

Reverse genetics

Mutate a gene. Observe the change in phenotype.

Genotype => Phenotype

Biochemical approach

Study proteins, enzyme activity, etc. to find out how and where the proteins are expressed.

Biochemists make assays of to measure how active an enzyme is.

Bioinformatic approach

Bioinformatics allows biologists to

  • look up databases to see if anyone else had already done a similar experiment,
  • find results from these similar experiments,
  • write algorithms to simulate biological environments.

In general, bioinformatics saves money by reducing the amount of lab work needed.

Model organisms

Benefits

  • cheap
  • short lifecycle - easy to observe entire generations / reproduction
  • transformable - easy to mutate its genes

Organism-specific benefits

  • S. Cerevisiae (yeast) has the benefit of being a rapidly-reproducing eukaryote that is cheap to maintain and "similar to humans" in terms of genome.
  • D. Melanogaster (fruit fly) is among the cheapest organisms for studying behaviour and development.
  • M. Musculus (house mouse) is really, really close to the human genome, and is relatively fast to clone.

Disadvantages of using model organisms

  • Findings on model organisms might not be reflected on humans, because humans are more complex.
